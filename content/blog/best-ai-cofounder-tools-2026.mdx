**TL;DR:** Polsia, Nanocorp, and Pancake are all trying to solve the same problem — letting very small teams run companies that used to require 10+ people. The difference is in the philosophy: Polsia and Nanocorp hand maximum control to the AI. Pancake keeps you in the driver's seat while multiplying your output. Here's what that looks like in practice.

---

## The problem all three are solving

A solo founder in 2026 is expected to do things that used to require a whole team: handle inbound leads, write and schedule content, manage a development roadmap, respond to customer support, track financials. Most people either burn out, hire before they're ready, or just drop the balls they can't carry.

Autonomous company platforms exist to change that math. The category is new — none of these products are more than two years old — and the approaches diverge in interesting ways.

---

## What Polsia and Nanocorp do well

Both platforms are designed for the maximum autonomy case: you describe what you want to build, and the AI runs as much of it as possible. If you're a solo founder who wants to get out of the way and let the system work, they're a real answer to a real problem.

Nanocorp's pitch is that you can launch a business with a single prompt. Polsia focuses more on the operational layer — automating workflows across a handful of integrated tools. Both are genuinely useful for founders who want to spend their time on strategy and let execution happen in the background.

The tradeoff is control. Both platforms require you to work inside their UI, which means your company lives inside their system. The agent behavior is a black box — you can't inspect what's running, modify how tasks are being handled, or hand off individual components to a different tool. If the platform makes a bad call, your options are limited.

---

## Where Pancake takes a different position

Pancake starts from a different assumption: a company is only yours if you actually built it. AI that runs 100% autonomously produces a company that belongs to the platform, not to you. The founder's judgment, taste, and instincts are what make a business worth building — and those can't be outsourced.

So Pancake's approach is guided autonomy. You work alongside AI agents. You provide direction, feedback, and approval on anything consequential. Over time, as the agents learn your preferences and your business, more and more gets handled without you. But you remain in the loop, and the company reflects your decisions.

That takes longer to initialize. Pancake isn't a "launch a business in five minutes" pitch. It's closer to hiring a team that you actually have to onboard — and then, once onboarded, works at a pace you couldn't match with human employees.

---

## The five practical differences

### 1. Solo vs. multiplayer

Polsia and Nanocorp are designed for one person. The interaction model assumes a single user giving instructions to a single system.

Pancake is multiplayer. Multiple co-founders can interact with the same agents simultaneously through Slack. A message from your CTO and a message from your head of sales both land in the same context, and the agents know who they're talking to. For any team with more than one person, this is a meaningful difference.

### 2. Their UI vs. your existing tools

Polsia and Nanocorp require you to work inside their platforms. Your workflow has to bend to their interface.

Pancake runs natively in Slack, iMessage, and email. You're already in Slack. Your co-founders are already in Slack. Your agents join your existing channels rather than pulling you into a new one. You can send a voice note on the way to a meeting and get a task handled. You can @mention your AI in a thread and it has the context of that thread.

### 3. Black box vs. open source

With Polsia and Nanocorp, you don't know what's running behind the product. The agent logic is proprietary. If something goes wrong, you get an error — not an explanation.

Pancake's sub-agents are open source and available on GitHub. Any agent can be inspected, forked, or replaced. The community builds skills and extensions. If you want to understand exactly how your outbound agent is writing emails, you can read the code. If you want to modify how it decides which leads to prioritize, you can. The system is yours in a way closed platforms can't be.

### 4. Credit system vs. transparent pricing

Polsia and Nanocorp use credits. You buy a bundle, the platform draws it down as tasks run. The problem is you never know exactly what each credit costs, which models are being used, or whether the system is making cost-saving substitutions that reduce quality.

Pancake charges $29/month for an always-on cloud computer, plus tokens billed at exactly the public rate from OpenAI and Anthropic. Our margin comes from the volume discount the labs give us — not from marking up your usage. You can see exactly what each task cost, which model ran it, and how many tokens were consumed.

### 5. Initialization time

Polsia and Nanocorp launch fast. That's part of their pitch.

Pancake takes longer to set up because the agents have to learn your company — your tone, your ICP, your existing processes, what's already been tried. The first week involves a real onboarding. After that, the agents work with more context than most human employees would have, because they've read every document, every Slack message, every meeting note you've connected.

The tradeoff is intentional. A company that was launched in five minutes reflects five minutes of thinking. Pancake is a bet on the opposite end of that spectrum.

## Which one is right for you

| | Pancake | Polsia | Nanocorp |
|---|---|---|---|
| Who controls decisions | You — guided by AI | Mostly AI | Almost entirely AI |
| Where you work | Slack, iMessage, email | Their UI | Their UI |
| Level of autonomy | Guided — you stay in the loop | High — AI runs most of it | Very high |
| Agent transparency | Open source on GitHub | Closed | Closed |
| Pricing model | Flat fee + tokens at cost | Credits | Credits |
| Multiplayer | Yes | No | No |
| Setup time | Days | Minutes | Minutes |

The real question isn't team size — it's how much control you want to keep.

If you want to hand execution to the AI and stay out of the way, Polsia or Nanocorp are built for that. You describe the goal; they run it.

If you want to remain the one making the calls — shaping strategy, approving direction, with AI multiplying your output rather than replacing your judgment — Pancake is built for you. Solo founders use it too. The difference is how much you want your fingerprints on the company.
